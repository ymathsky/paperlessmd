From the Patients section, click Add Patient.

The form has everything in one place. First and last name, date of birth — important for duplicate checking and Practice Fusion matching. Phone, email, and address — the address pre-fills into every form so the Medical Assistant never types it again.

Insurance carrier and Member ID are separate fields so the ID is always machine-readable for billing.

You can upload the insurance card right here — front and back. The thumbnail appears instantly. Staff can pull this up on any device during a visit.

The pharmacy section is one of the biggest time-savers in the system. Enter the pharmacy name, phone number, and address once. From this point forward, every visit form and every consent pre-fills the pharmacy automatically. Set it once, never type it again. Same for the PCP.

Select the assigned Medical Assistant from the dropdown — this patient then appears on that Medical Assistant's patient list and schedule, not everyone else's.

Click Save. You're taken straight to the patient's profile, ready to go.

Every patient has a profile that serves as their complete clinical record inside PaperlessMD. Let me walk you through each tab using Betty Johnson as our example.

At the top is the summary section — name, date of birth, insurance, key contact info. Below that are documents on file: insurance card front and back, and a government ID photo. Click any thumbnail and it opens full screen in a lightbox. Tap anywhere to close it.

The Forms tab shows every form ever submitted for this patient — the date, form type, which Medical Assistant submitted it, and the current status: Draft or Signed. Click the PDF icon to open a print-ready version.

The Medications tab is the master medication list. Every active medication is listed with the name, frequency, and type — Maintenance, Refill, New, or Discontinued. When a provider discontinues a medication, it stops pre-filling into future forms automatically. Click the clock icon on any medication to see the full edit history — who changed what and when.

Vitals History shows trend charts for blood pressure, pulse, oxygen saturation, and weight. Each point on the chart is a visit. Hover over any point to see the exact reading and date. Scroll down for the raw data table — every vital from every visit, newest first.

Wounds tracks measurements per wound site — length, width, depth — with calculated area and a trend showing whether the wound is improving or worsening over time.

Photos groups all wound images by site, with a side-by-side compare mode so you can show healing progression between two dates. That's a powerful visual for insurance documentation.

Diagnoses lists active ICD-10 codes. Add or remove codes here and they stay in sync with the visit forms.

SOAP Notes is where providers draft and finalize clinical notes. The AI can generate a draft from a completed visit form — I'll show you that shortly.

Care Notes is for coordination threads — messages between staff about this patient's care. Things like "called the pharmacy about a prior authorization, waiting on a callback." These stay attached to the patient's record permanently.

And the Audit tab — admin only — shows every single action taken on this patient. Who viewed the profile, who edited a medication, who submitted a form. Timestamped and IP-logged. If a compliance auditor asks who accessed this record on a specific date, you have the answer in seconds.

Now let me walk you through the New Patient Packet — this is one of the most important workflows in PaperlessMD, and one of the biggest time-savers you will see today.

The New Patient Packet is the consent and intake section that every new patient completes on their very first visit. It contains three required documents: the CCM enrollment consent, the Advance Beneficiary Notice, and the Illinois PHI disclosure authorization.

Here is what makes this powerful. The moment you open the packet, all three forms are already filled in. The patient's name, date of birth, address, insurance carrier, Member ID, pharmacy name, pharmacy phone number, and pharmacy address — every field that would normally take five to ten minutes to type — is already there. The system pulled it all automatically from the patient profile that was set up when the patient was added.

The Medical Assistant does not type a single thing. They open the packet, turn the screen toward the patient, and walk them through each consent one at a time. The progress bar at the top shows exactly which step you are on and how many remain. Each consent is its own step — CCM enrollment first, then the Advance Beneficiary Notice, then the Illinois PHI disclosure.

For each form, the Medical Assistant reads the key points with the patient, answers any questions, and clicks Next to advance. The patient is never handed a clipboard with a stack of papers. Everything happens on one device, in one workflow, in the right order.

When all three consents are complete, the signature step opens. The patient signs directly on the canvas with a finger on a tablet or a mouse on a desktop. The signature is large, clear, and captured at full resolution. If a family member or guardian is signing on behalf of the patient, flip the POA toggle — fields appear immediately for the guardian's name and their relationship to the patient.

Below the patient signature is the Medical Assistant block. If the Medical Assistant has saved their signature in their profile settings, you will see the Auto-fill ON badge — the signature appears automatically without them having to draw it again. They can toggle it off and sign manually on any individual form if needed.

The provider signature block works the same way. If the provider is present at the visit, they sign here and the packet is complete on the spot. If the provider is not on-site — which is typical in home-based care — the packet is submitted and immediately appears in the provider's sign queue. The provider opens it on their phone, reviews the completed forms, and countersigns in under a minute from anywhere.

Click Submit and Save. Three legally signed consent documents are captured, timestamped, and permanently attached to the patient's record the instant that button is pressed. They appear immediately on the Forms tab. The PDF is ready to print or share. No paper. No scanning. No driving back to the office to upload anything.

The sign queue is where providers countersign forms submitted by Medical Assistants.

Every unsigned form appears here — the date it was submitted, the patient name, the form type, and which Medical Assistant submitted it. Click a form to open a read-only view of the entire completed form. Scroll to the provider signature block and click Sign.

If the provider has a saved signature, it auto-fills. Otherwise they draw it here. Click Save Signature.

The form disappears from the queue and updates to Signed status in the patient's record — with the provider's name and the exact countersign timestamp.

The provider doesn't need to be at the visit. They review and sign from their phone between patients. The whole process takes under a minute from any device, anywhere.

The schedule gives every Medical Assistant their daily route and gives admins a real-time view of the whole team.

In day view, each card is a scheduled visit — patient name, address, phone number, visit type, and time. The status badge shows where each visit stands: Pending, En Route, Completed, or Missed. Tap the badge to update it in one tap. The admin sees that change in real time.

Tap the notes icon on any visit card to add or view visit notes — useful for recording building access codes, patient instructions, or anything the next Medical Assistant needs to know.

Toggle to Week view for the full Monday through Sunday grid. Each day shows the visit count. Click any day to jump to that day's detail view.

Admins have a Medical Assistant filter at the top — select any Medical Assistant to see only their schedule, or All Medical Assistants to see the full team in one view.

From Manage Schedule, admins can add visits, reorder them by dragging to optimize the route, and delete visits with a confirmation step.

For patients seen on a regular cadence — weekly wound care, monthly follow-ups — use Recurring Schedule. Set the patient, Medical Assistant, visit type, day of week, and frequency. Set a start date and either an end date or a maximum occurrence count. The schedule generates itself going forward. You set it once and never touch it again.

PaperlessMD has a built-in messaging system so your team never needs to share patient information over personal text messages.

The Messages section has two panels — the chat list on the left, the conversation on the right. At the top of the list is All Staff — a broadcast channel. Any message you send here goes to every logged-in staff member at once.

Click any staff member for a private conversation. Messages appear in real time — no page reload needed. All messages are timestamped in the clinic's timezone, not the Medical Assistant's device timezone, so there's no confusion about when something was sent.

Click the paperclip icon to attach wound photos, PDFs, or any file directly in the chat.

The notification bell in the top bar shows your unread count. Click it to open the notification drawer — unread messages, forms waiting on a provider signature, and any old drafts flagged for your attention.

No texting patient information on personal phones. All clinical communication stays here — logged, searchable, and HIPAA-safe.

PaperlessMD includes an AI assistant purpose-built for wound care documentation. Click the blue pulse icon in the bottom-right corner to open it.

First — clinical coding. Ask it a plain-English question: "What ICD-10 code should I use for a stage three pressure ulcer on the left heel?" It returns the exact code with the clinical description and documentation tips. Click Copy to copy the answer to your clipboard.

Second — SOAP note drafting. Ask it to draft a SOAP note for a patient. It returns a structured Subjective, Objective, Assessment, and Plan — ready for the provider to review and finalize. Click Insert into SOAP and it goes directly to the patient's SOAP Notes tab.

Third — clinical wound descriptions. Ask it to write a clinical description for a wound with specific dimensions and characteristics. It returns a ready-to-paste clinical narrative — exactly what belongs in a referral or progress note.

Fourth — billing questions. Ask about CPT codes, what documentation supports a specific claim, or what the Medicare coverage criteria are. It handles all of it.

The AI receives only de-identified clinical text — no patient names, no identifying information. It's purpose-built for wound care. Not a generic chatbot.

Now let me show you everything the administrator can do — because this is where the practice owner or office manager spends most of their time in PaperlessMD.

From the admin panel, click Staff to manage your team. The staff list shows every user — full name, role badge, last login timestamp, and an active-inactive toggle. If someone leaves the practice, click the toggle. They cannot log in from that moment forward, but every form they ever submitted and every record they ever touched is preserved. Nothing is deleted. To add a new staff member, click Add Staff. Enter their full name and email, then choose their role from six color-coded cards. Medical Assistant in indigo — they see their assigned patients, submit forms, and update visit statuses. Provider in teal — they get the sign queue, full clinical access, and can finalize SOAP notes. Scheduler in violet — schedule management only, no clinical data. PCC in teal — patient care coordination access. Billing in amber — they see submitted forms and ICD codes, but cannot access vitals, wound photos, or clinical notes. Admin in red — full access to everything in the system.

The password strength bar updates in real time as you type. Red for weak, yellow for moderate, green for very strong. Use the eye icon to show or hide the password before saving.

Under Roles and Permissions, the full access matrix shows exactly what each role can and cannot do. This is not access that is hidden behind a warning — it is access that simply does not exist for that role. A billing user cannot navigate to wound photos. The page does not appear in their menu. It is structurally absent. This makes HIPAA compliance straightforward — the access boundaries are enforced by the system, not by policy alone.

Admins also control the patient roster. From any patient profile, the admin can reassign the patient to a different Medical Assistant, edit the patient's insurance information, update the pharmacy, upload or replace the insurance card photos and government ID, and discharge a patient when care ends. Discharged patients are removed from active lists but remain fully accessible in the records.

The admin manages the full schedule. Add individual visits or set up recurring schedules for patients on a regular cadence. Drag and drop to reorder visits within a day and optimize the route. Delete visits with a confirmation step to prevent accidental removal.

Admins also have access to the Audit Log — the most powerful compliance tool in the system. Every single action taken by every user is recorded here. Who logged in, who viewed a patient profile, who submitted a form, who edited a medication, who changed a password — all of it logged with the exact timestamp and IP address. Filter by staff member, by action type, or by date range. Export the results to a CSV file for compliance submissions, insurance audits, or internal reviews. If a question ever comes up about what happened on a specific date with a specific patient, the audit log has the answer.
